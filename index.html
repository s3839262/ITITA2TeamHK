As a software developer, there are 5 main steps.


First is building the feature. This is the part where you receive a brief from your client, and possibly some quick thumbnail designs on the final product. Everything is basically a guideline here, and you start off working with respect towards the initial idea, but remain open to the fact that the final product will most likely look extremely different to the first concept.

Next, addressing all of the immediate issues, finding bugs, determining which ones are the highest priority and require fixing. This may require additional learning, either as a result of bugs that are beyond your initial scope of knowledge, or through code combing highlighting the need for tuning and refinement in your technique.

Finally, you speak with your client/team to see how satisfied they are with your final outcome, push your project to live, then work with your team/client to dig into technical difficulties - what went wrong, why it broke, and how to fix it.

In other words, it is constant trial and error.

You will communicate often with your clients, first so you can get an idea of what the core functionality of your design should be, and then through constant feedback and discussion, develop further iterations of your initial work that will be closer to what your client had in mind.  Oftentimes you’ll be working with teams, especially for large companies that will demand equally large projects that simply couldn’t be completed alone in a practical timeframe.

Challenges in the field include the demands of people who make extremely ambitious demands due to their lack of knowledge in the technical aspect of  software engineering. The culture of the profession can also be hostile - you may face elitists and gatekeepers amongst those who do consider software development to be their field of expertise. You will also find a financial motive being shoehorned into passion projects, which can distort the initial idea into something entirely business driven. This can be draining for those who do software development because they enjoy creating and designing their projects.

Kerry Lam - Machine Learning

Machine learning teaches AI about teaching itself, learning without constant (read: 24/7) supervision and correction from humans. By teaching it to develop an understanding of the relationship between the terms it comes across, an AI can draw from its previous “experiences” through pattern recognition. Through this, an AI can develop and flesh out a program on its own once given a founding set of instructions or goal from human hands. In some ways, the overarching concept could be compared to the way a declarative programming language works: You give the system a goal, and the system works towards that goal without you having to provide instructions on how the goal is achieved.

Currently, the strengths of machine learning seem to be quite clear: while machines and computers easily trump humans in areas of logic, fields that require more abstract thinking are much more elusive to grasp. For instance, teaching a computer to read and comprehend an image the same way a human would is surprisingly difficult. Humans can easily recognise objects that are depicted in an abstract manner, whereas computers will often take the image far too literally. Where a human can immediately learn to understand that a cartoon traffic light is still a traffic light, a computer that hasn’t had enough time to develop its database on the features that define a traffic light may only see a gray rectangle with three coloured circles inscribed upon it. 


However, although it is difficult and expensive at the moment, this is not an entirely insurmountable hill for machine learning to overcome. A common technique used to bypass the obscurity and ambiguity of images is to implement tags, words that are consistent and uniform and thus far easier to recognise patterns in, and generate conclusions from. Even pure/raw images are not impossible to work with, and some highly developed artificial intelligence are capable of generating extremely convincing images of human faces, complete with expressions that are real enough to fool a human viewer. 


Machine Learning, ultimately, is a means for humanity to surpass the limits of their own minds. Computers and machines, when you put aside their current flaws, learn at an incredible speed, and their growth can move at an exponential rate. Kerry Lam - Autonomous vehicles


Autonomous vehicles, as the name would suggest, refer to vehicles that require no driver to bring passengers from one destination to another. There is a relevant branch revolving around systems of public transport that work without drivers or human operators, though generally the term refers to bodies that have a system completely localised to the specific vehicle, much like your typical car in traffic (autonomy aside).


All current (or relevant, at least) designs for autonomous vehicles rely on sensors on the vehicle that act as the car’s eyes. The primary functions of the vehicle’s sensors are to detect nearby objects, to recognise the type of object, and to determine the rate at which the distance between the car and any given object changes.

The need to detect nearby objects is obvious enough - an autonomous car with no way to detect its environment is simply a car accident (or a dozen) just waiting to happen. The car needs to recognise the type of object it is approaching as well. It needs to recognise pedestrians and most likely animals as to prevent injury where possible, both to those inside and outside of the car. It also needs to understand typical forms of physical communication and instructions, such as road signs and traffic lights. Finally, determining the rate at which an object is approaching will help the vehicle adjust its current path and/or speed in order to minimise harm to all who are involved. 


Additionally, the vehicle will require AI and machine learning to have the “brain” to process all of this data constantly, and it has to do it accurately. The AI needs to understand what the correct decision is based on different road conditions, such as weather, or routes that have been blocked off. 


Currently, there is a limited amount of faith in this technology. Most companies are extremely reluctant to push their designs out for public use, and that will most likely be the case for a long time to come. This is due to certain weaknesses of AI and general information technology that may be hard to control and predict the onset of. 


In normal circumstances, any program is bound to have bugs, and it is not unexpected for fatal errors to occur. What happens when such an issue happens in the context of autonomous vehicles? Such a bug could be quite literally fatal, or at the very least lead to a complete halt of traffic systems in the scenario where autonomous vehicles become a prevalent form of technology for public use. Things like a failure on the AI’s ability to recognise certain road states and conditions, or even a complete crash of the system, or a faulty update - these things can easily lead to the deaths of countless people if the system isn’t perfected. Even if the bugs are minor, the efficiency of an autonomous car would be expected to match the efficiency of a human driver at least.

However, once it does take off, it has the potential to dominate and overtake the driver/chauffeur industry. In a perfect world, the autonomous vehicle wouldn’t merely match a human driver; it would move faster, yet it would be far safer than the most experienced of human drivers. The average human’s ping, often referred to as ‘reaction time’ (which isn’t nearly as funny, quite frankly) is around a quarter of a second. Various factors, such as the mental state of the driver, can impact this. Not only will a machine have a much crisper reaction time, it will not suffer from distractions. The operation of the vehicle itself will be performed in a much safer manner.

Transportation in general will be more accessible for everyone. People who have certain conditions that make it dangerous for them to drive, yet require the services of a car will find autonomous vehicles to be a blessing. Conditions like vision impairment, narcolepsy, trauma related to driving, or old age will no longer gate an individual’s access to the outside world, especially if they live on their own, or with others who cannot drive. Even those who live with someone who can drive them will be thankful for an option that is always up regardless of the availability of their usual driver.

The introduction of autonomous driving would be a massive boon for us, even though we travel fairly minimal distances. The impact that this could have in places where buses and general public transportation are not as prevalent or effective would be immense - allowing the world to move faster, and more effectively.

Hansel Chang - Cloud Services, Servers


What is the cloud? The cloud is basically several servers connected to the internet and chartered as a part of a software or an application service. The cloud server is a virtual server run via a cloud computing environment. It's made available to customers from the providers' servers, which ultimately means companies do not have to host their server on their on-premises servers. Before cloud services, companies would need to have their own infrastructures, causing it to be very costly to host something, and often, with heavy traffic, their servers would not be able to handle it and would eventually crash. Cloud servers are very convenient because they can be accessed remotely; as they are built, hosted and delivered all via the internet through a platform. These are commonly used for web hosting, data sharing and hosting, and for software or application use. Before cloud services, doing tasks mentioned were very time-consuming, expensive and unreliable. Cloud services are composed to help render easy, affordable access to resources, without the need for infrastructures. Cloud services are used way more often than realised, as from checking email to downloading a picture, everything is from a cloud, and most of us do not realise it. A significant advantage of cloud storage is the fact that many scattered resources are acting as one; these are called federated storage clouds. It makes the cloud tolerant to flaws and faults due to the dissemination of data. Cloud servers are stable, fast and secure. Budgets can be kept at a bare minimum with the help of cloud services, as businesses can avoid paying extra for I.T. support, mostly since hardware issues often occur with physical servers. Having a cloud service will help provide a much quicker service as opposed to a physical server, not to mention the physical server would probably cost more to host. A lot of cloud services today are very competitive and would have excellent support for free to make sure your use is up and running 24/7. Project Ideas



Over the course of the COVID-19 crisis, a certain issue has come to our notice - the lack of support for those who are not fortunate enough to have the money we have, who struggle to afford the newly added items to the list of daily essentials. Things like masks and hand sanitisers, we understand, may not be easy to come across for our own use, let alone to gather enough surplus that we could happily give away large amounts of it to strangers. However, there are still the usual essentials, things that are still vital for sustaining ourselves even outside of the COVID-19 crisis - food. 


Our project, Freat, would be an effective platform for addressing this issue. The aim of Freat is to host a large market for people to purchase food at a significantly lower price or for free. Businesses such as restaurants or shops can supply foods that they may not necessarily want to put on their shelves, even if it is still safe to eat. Things like produce that have abnormal appearances, which have no effect on the taste or nutritional value, or even food that a business has a notable surplus of, can be put up on Freat on a heavy discount, or even be made completely free. Partnerships may include food delivery services such as UberEats and Deliveroo.


The aim of the app is to make contributions and support towards the worldwide crisis of poverty, as well as helping our fellow Australians in our own neighbourhoods during this extreme time of need. This app will be particularly beneficial to those who are currently homeless and have access to a mobile phone. 


The name Freat is an amalgamation of the words Free and Eat. To reflect this, it is expected that participating businesses would be willing to supply food for minimal prices, and even for free. However, we do understand the current circumstances of COVID-19 and struggling businesses; that's why we believe it is fair for businesses to charge a small amount to help them out as well. 


However, our ultimate goal is for Freat to assist our communities, rather than to extract profit from them. We would ask partnered companies to either list food up for free or for a bare minimum amount of up to 80% off of its original price depending on the condition of the food being supplied. 


This app would also be an excellent opportunity for participating businesses who would like to reinforce their reputation by providing to the community. It would provide them with further exposure and thus increase customer base growth and retention. Additionally, it could still generate minor profit for them, as they would be selling food that normally might never leave the shelf at all before the end of its lifespan.


For our project, we decided to consider how we could improve our current social climate and living standards, and started by looking at the bigger picture, seeing what issues were at a global scale. We felt that there should be more apps developed with altruistic intent, for the sake of bettering communities rather than being financial/business oriented. 


One of the biggest global challenges that are yet to be solved is food security. Australia, with its population of 25 million people, generates over 5 million tonnes of food wastage every year, costing the economy roughly 20 billion dollars in that timeframe. The issue becomes even more horrifying when you consider Australia’s population is only 0.3% of the global headcount. 


In response to this, we came up with Freat, which provides benefits for both its target audience and the partnering businesses. Businesses wouldn't have to worry about wasting food, knowing that it would be sent to someone who needs it, and users walk away with free food or a good bargain at the very least. 


Although we know an issue as ancient and far reaching as world food security is far beyond the scope of Freat, we hope we can at least help our neighbours. We believe that there needs to be more awareness raised for food security, as according to WeForum, the world will have to feed almost 10 billion people, which is a 30 percent increase from today’s demands. The amount of food wastage this will generate if not addressed is unfathomable. Malnutrition, poverty, even pollution can be tied to food wastage. 


Right now, times are particularly dire with the pressures of the COVID-19 pandemic, even for those who may not normally face any of these struggles. With employment rates becoming so unstable, it’s hard to tell who may suddenly find the rug being pulled from under their feet in just a few months, weeks, or even days. Government schemes can only do so much, and these people will need support for a long time after the complete withdrawal of the COVID supplement and JobKeeper payments.


Freat will be a platform primarily marketed as a mobile application, available on iOS and Android. We do understand that those who require our service may not necessarily have access to a mobile phone, therefore, a web version will be made available. However, actions on the web version may be limited as opposed to the app. For this report, we will be describing the app version. 


When first entering the app, the app will request the user to enable location services. This will allow the app to get the exact location of the user and provide a selection of businesses that are offering deals on food. 


Users will have access to the app as a guest, but can only receive food if they have registered and logged into a Freats account. This bit of flexibility will allow for users to get a preview of what they can access if they partake in Freats. We intend for the registration process to be extremely brief, with no limitations or qualifications required for an account. There will be a small banner on the top of the app, featuring and promoting partnered businesses. This should be the only advertisement visible on the platform, and will act as a source of income for app maintenance. 


As there may be a vast selection of food to “purchase”, a filter and search option will be available, allowing users to have a bit more control over what they take, with the default setting being based on proximity to the user. For purchasers, there will be a clear distinction between the free food and the discounted food. There will be an option to browse from other locations if the user wishes to expand their horizons, or are willing to travel a bit further for a particular deal. Upon purchasing, users are to reserve and collect the order within a specific timeframe. Any users caught abusing the system will be permanently banned from the platform, and device ID will be collected, as to restrict their access to Freats.


The companies’ version of the app will provide much more flexibility as compared to the user app. It will give them options to list food and halt supply any time. Photos are not required, but they are encouraged to provide images of the food they are supplying. For the businesses, there is an option to declare a limit to how much food each person can take or purchase, along with an overall total supply limit. We would aim to minimise the costs of food from Freats, and businesses will have to provide the original price of the product for legal purposes, ensuring that users of Freats are not being tricked into paying more than they should have to. We won't charge business anything for free Freats, and to encourage that, we will be charging non-free Freats for a small percentage per order. Businesses will need to provide reasons as to why they are offering the food for free or for cheap - if they are expiring soon, they are required to provide the expiry date. 


Freats aims to provide for the community, and there is also a legal obligation to pay attention to our user's wellbeing, especially after consuming food that was purchased through Freats. We do not want to see users eating food that has long expired for the sake of frugality. An in-built chat system will be in the system for all platforms, to streamline communications between users and parties. 


As the userbase grows, the amount of free food available will decrease unless businesses keep up with it. We would try to counteract this by implementing a reward system for businesses. The more they give away, the more progress they gain. By the end of the month, depending on availabilities within relevant regions, we would donate a percentage of profits from non-free food to charities associated with food security, and attribute the donations to the winning businesses. 


The nascence of our app would rely on a tool called Invision to help us map out the main U.I. of Freat. We want to keep Freat looking as clean and straightforward as possible, as we do expect our userbase to be primarily composed of middle-aged and older, who may be less familiar with using mobile applications. To accommodate our users is vital, and so we would aim for a minimalistic interface to prevent a hostile user experience. A short training or tutorial on how to use the app will be provided.


The development will consist of two sectors; one for users and one for businesses. A specialised tutorial will be provided for proxies acting on behalf of partnering businesses and companies. The mobile version of Freat will rely on Xcode for iOS and open-source tools such as Android Studio for Android. Knowledge in popular languages such as Objective-C and Swift will be required in order to compile this app. Upon finishing, App testing tools and emulators will  be used to help make sure everything is functional. One great emulator widely used would be Appium; which is compatible with both Android and iOS apps. Our website version will heavily require HTML designs as well as backend PHP coding, as this will require a lot of instructions to the system. Bear in mind, both the app and the website have to connect to the same server, to make sure the data of all users and businesses locations are on the same page. 


The skills required for Freat would include going through testing the app; this includes going through countless rounds of trial and error to develop the app. This may consist of all sorts of code from Swift to Objective-C. Additionally, understanding how to use development tools, and confidence in collaborating with other developers, ability to design and cross-platform development will be vital for making Freat run nominally. 


As of now, we both lack the skills in terms of coding, and would probably need to finish up our I.T. or Computer Science degree to meet the requirements to complete this project. These are definitely achievable, but will require effort to properly dedicate various programming languages to memory so that it can be implemented effectively. It will be useful to have both a Mac and Windows, as both hardware give different benefits in terms of coding. One being more superior in terms of coding for Android (personal preference), and one being the only hardware to allow you to officially code for iOS - the Mac. 


The great news is that most software are open-source and free to use, so apart from the price for the knowledge learned; no additional costs are required to code this project. 


Poverty has been an issue ever since man first learnt how to record history, but we believe our current world is more than wealthy enough, abundant enough in resources to address this if we take proper action instead of letting it be. 


The outcome for this project would not only bring a functional app, but will also bring joy to many families suffering in our communities, possibly providing inspiration for other people to take similar action elsewhere, and overall spread awareness of the issue and the common person’s power to change the world. 


Freat, while small on its own, would still help by feeding families and supporting businesses. In addition, this will reduce food wastage, leading to less landfill, and less resources wasted on creating that food in the first place. 


Who knows how many times there has been an item has been denied a place in a supermarket shelf, or in someone’s pantry, even when it was perfectly fine to eat. With the ongoing demand for fresh and aesthetically pleasing food, the reject gets tossed away at night when someone who needs it would happily take it, regardless of how ugly it looked, or if it was just a little bit dry or stale. This will help ease some of the pains of those who struggled even before the COVID-19 crisis, let alone now, as well as help those without a job or home.
